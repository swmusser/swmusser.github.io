'Proving' Euler's formula to calculus students

Jan 11, 2019 2 min read

Back in high school when I took AP Calc BC I encountered integration using partial fractions. Later, while fooling around with the idea I realized I could use it to connect trigonometric functions and imaginary numbers. I didn’t think much of it at the time, but shortly thereafter I learned of Euler’s formula and realized that I had stumbled upon a way to “prove” this relation using integration techniques familiar to most students of calculus. (While researching the proof for this post I discovered that Johann Bernoulli nearly completed a similar “proof” in the 18th century, so the idea was just a new one to me.) Afterwards I demonstrated the idea to my intro calc class at UChicgo, which some of them appreciated. I will reproduce the argument here, as I expect some will find it amusing and may want to show it to their own undergraduate students.

As any student of calculus will be familiar with $$\begin{eqnarray} \tan^{-1}(x) &=& \left[\tan^{-1}(y) \right]_0^{x} \\
&=& \int_0^{x} \frac{1}{1+y^2} \ \mathrm{d}y. \end{eqnarray}$$ If we now use the difference of squares to see that $ 1+y^2 = (1+iy)(1-iy) $, then we can split up the integrand into the sum of two fractions using the technique of partial fractions. So therefore: $$\begin{eqnarray} \tan^{-1}(x) &=& \int_0^{x} \frac{1}{1+y^2} \ \mathrm{d}y \\
&=& \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{x} \left[\frac{1}{1+iy} + \frac{1}{1-iy}\right] \ \mathrm{d}y \\
&=& \frac{i}{2}\left[\ln(1 - iy) - \ln(1 + iy) \right]_0^{x} \\
&=& \frac{i}{2}\ln\left(\frac{1-iy}{1+iy}\right). \end{eqnarray}$$ Our reasoning has been slightly dubious here, namely we’ve ignored the multi-valued nature of the complex logarithm. That’s probably not a hugely important distinction for those just learning calculus, however, so we forge onward.
